Introduction¶

A travel company in India is selling a travel insurance package. The company would like to automate its marketing efforts for the product and improve their sales forecasting by using an ML model which predicts if a travel client will buy the insurance based on the sociodemographic data available about that client.

Since there is no information about whether the travel insurance is for domestic travel or travel abroad, I am assuming that it is covering both - domestic travel and travel abroad.

Business Objectives¶

This model will be used in:

  1. automated marketing to the existing client database
  2. product sales forecasting, thus product pricing

Both functions are important to the business because of the revenue generation potential:

  1. Mistakes in #1 will lead to missed revenue (if the right clients are not reached) and/or clients annoyed with irrelevant marketing (if the wrong clients are reached).
  2. Mistakes in #2 could be very detrimental to the business because with expected more policyholders, the risks are expected to be spread out over a larger pool which reduces variance/volatility in claims experience and could allow charging lower required premiums. But misjudging the sales potential and not reaching the estimated sales goals, would cause losses to the company. On the other side underestimating the sales potential would mean missed revenue.

Modelling type and goals¶

The goal is to create a supervised learning classification ML model, that can predict if a particular customer will buy the travel insurance packaged based on the sociodemographic information about that client and the existing labeled examples.

The data is small enough to do a standard batch learning.

It is assumed that the prediction results will be used in a format of categories (1 for likely to buy, 0 not likely to buy) and the precise probability is not of interest here. If a model returns a precise probability the rounding of probability results is done as follows: >= 0.5 is rounded to 1, everything else to 0.

Performance measures:

  • Maximize reaching the right clients which will buy
  • Minimize reaching the wrong clients who will not buy
  • Mistakes in overestimating sales potential (treating wrong clients as right, i.e. willing to buy) for the sales forecasting are the most costly financially, because it not only impacts the revenue, but also the product pricing decisions.
  • Mistakes in underestimating sales potential (treating right clients as wrong, i.e. unwilling to buy) are also costly in missed potential revenue, but not as costly as overestimating.

Including all the binary variables as well, because I found a discussion on stats.stackexchange mentioning that for binary data Pearson gives an equivalent of the Phi coefficient which the usually suggested association measure for binary data.

A * next to the value indicates significance based on p-value (P<0.005) - this is intentionally 10 times smaller than the usual 0.05 cut-off point, because larger sample sizes generally lead to smaller p-values (statistical tests were originally designed for samples in tens and hundreds, not thousands).

Choosing the model shortlist¶

In this section I am trying out multiple models to see which perform the best for selection into the shortlist of the best models to test on the test dataset. Since the dataset is not too large (running pipelines multiple times is fast), I combine trying out different models with their hyperparameter tuning into one automated code, which does the following:

  • it accepts a list of models and a list of parameter grids for GridSearchCV,
  • prepares data split for 5 fold cross-validation, the split is done using StratifiedGroupKFold, because there are 'duplicate' values in the data
  • evaluates each model by running it through the 5 fold cross-validation, using grid search inside each fold
  • for each model each fold it returns the best model hyperparameters and saves mean performance scores for a summary comparison

In summary we have high precision, low recall: the models are conservative in making positive predictions. They only predict positive category when being quite confident, but miss a substantial number of actual positive cases. All models 'suffer' from this to some extent. From the business objective perspective:

  • having a conservative model is OK in case it is used for product pricing decisions where overestimating market size might and approving lower prices could lead to large losses if the market size actually does not materialize.
  • however if the model is used only in marketing, it would lead to missing out on many potential insurance buyers.

Using algorithms that are robust to imbalanced datasets (3 LogisticRegression, 5 SVC and 8 RandomForestClassifier) does:

  • balance precision and recall somewhat, but does not improve the model overall
  • makes SVC model worse
  • marginally improve the overall performance with RandomForestClassifier

Conclusions¶

Data suitability:

  • The data provides a large dataset for modeling.
  • However there are many duplicate observations and some of them are leading to conflicting labels. This is impacting the split of the data into train/test and overall impacts the model performance.
  • TravelInsurance classes are imbalanced with fewer 1s than 0s. This is impacting the model by making it 'pessimistic' in predicting the positive class.
  • Other binary features are also imbalanced.
  • There are some correlations between features, especially AnnualIncome, but the correlations are not very strong.
  • Different variables are on very different scales.

Model selection:

  • From a variety of tested models Random Forest Classifier performs the best.
  • No single model was able to surpass this result.
  • No other tested ensemble was able to surpass this result.
  • All the tested models and ensembles were suffering from the same issue with recall performance.

Random Forest Classifier model performance:

  • Model has training accuracy 0.81, precision 0.82, recall 0.59, f1 0.69, roc_auc 0.76.
  • Model test performance is: accuracy 0.79, precision 0.71, recall 0.61, f1 0.66, roc_auc 0.74. It is similar to the train performance indicating that the model is generalizing reasonably well on unseen data. However the testing precision is visibly worse in the test data.
  • This is better than chance, but is not an impressive performance as far as the ML models go.
  • Recall is especially an issue - the model is very conservative in calling the positive class, thus missing a lot of positives.
  • Trying to raise recall very quickly decreases precision and other metrics substantially as well to the point of the model becoming pointless.

Practical business application:

  • If the business would use this model for insurance pricing decisions, possibly low recall and being sure about the positive class predictions is good in a sense that pricing decisions based on the estimated sales potential would not be likely to backfire. It is better to underestimate the sales potential when making pricing decisions, than to overestimate it.
  • However if the business would use this model for sales targeting (such as promotional emails), the low recall would lead to missed sales opportunities (and missed revenue) because people who are likely to buy, often would be classified as not interested.
